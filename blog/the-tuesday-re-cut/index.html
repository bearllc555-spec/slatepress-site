The Tuesday re-cut.

Why we re-cut the first three slides of every active deck on Tuesday mornings — what it catches, and why most agencies never do it.

SLATEPRESS · April 23, 2026 · 5 min read

Every Tuesday morning, before any new work begins, we reopen every active deck in the studio and re-cut the first three slides. Cover, problem, one-line-what-we-do. Nothing else. One hour, every deck, every week.

It started as a discipline hack. A founder had pushed back on slide two of her deck on a Friday, we agreed with the critique, and by Monday the edit had slipped down the list behind a newer project. By Tuesday the deck was in front of a partner. The critique was real and it was still there. So we put the cadence on the calendar and it hasn't moved since.

The rule is narrow on purpose: only the first three slides, only on Tuesdays, every active deck in the room. No exceptions for "it's fine" or "the founder approved it last week." The first three slides are the only ones most investors read closely, and they drift the fastest as a round evolves.

What the re-cut actually is

One designer and one writer sit with the deck for ten to fifteen minutes. The deck goes on a screen large enough that type size, image crop, and composition are all legible at the same time. The three slides get a fresh read from the top. Not an edit pass. A reread.

Then three questions, in order. Does the cover answer what the business is in one sentence, or does it still lean on the company name and a tagline? Does the problem slide state a real claim about the customer's current life, or a tidy abstraction? Does the third slide land the "what we do" without burying it under feature language?

If any of the three answers is shaky, we re-cut the slide on the spot and send the updated deck back to the founder by end of day. If all three are clean, the deck moves on. The whole pass takes an hour for a studio running five active decks. It is the single highest-yield hour of our week.

Why the first three slides, and no others

Most investors will read the first three slides carefully, skim the middle, and scrutinize the last two. If the first three don't hold, the middle is irrelevant. Every other slide is a downstream problem. Fix the doorway and the rest of the house gets evaluated on its merits.

The re-cut is also narrow because the first three slides are the ones the founder has looked at a hundred times and stopped seeing. The team has read the cover so often that it no longer parses. A reader arriving fresh on Tuesday morning can see what the team no longer can. That fresh read is the product; the edit is just the delivery.

What it catches

A surprising amount. The cover line that made sense three weeks ago has fallen out of step with the new positioning and now reads as a line from an earlier version of the company. The problem slide is two beats too long and buries the claim. The third slide describes the product with a feature list when what the round is really selling is a new category.

It also catches drift the founder hasn't noticed. An investor dropped an objection on Monday. A word changed in the cap table model. A new customer logo wants to be on the cover. None of those things would justify a standalone revision — they just quietly change what the first three slides should be saying. The Tuesday pass pulls them up.

Sharp edge

Most deck agencies never re-cut a shipped deck. Once the final file is delivered, it leaves the shop and the shop moves on to the next project. That model made sense when a deck took six weeks to build and nothing moved during the raise. In 2026, decks are living documents that get re-read weekly by partners who are forming a picture of the round. An agency that doesn't keep cutting is delivering a static artifact into a dynamic process.

Why most agencies don't do it

Economics. Re-cutting after delivery doesn't fit inside a fixed-scope engagement priced on a six-week Gantt chart. It requires a relationship that stays open through the raise, and a cost structure light enough to absorb recurring hours without charging for each one. Traditional agencies are neither.

We can run the Tuesday re-cut because the base cost of a slide revision in our shop is a tenth of what it was two years ago. Taste and framing are still the expensive inputs; the mechanical work of re-cutting a slide is now fast. That shift is what makes an ongoing cadence affordable on our end and invisible on the founder's. The deck improves in the background.

How to run a version on your own deck

A founder can run a solo version of this. Put Tuesday morning on the calendar. Open only the first three slides. Ask the three questions above, out loud, before reading the slides. Then read the slides and see where the gap is. The gap is almost always there. The trick is that most weeks, nobody is looking at those slides with fresh eyes — so nobody sees it.

The deck doesn't need a big rewrite between raises. It needs a small, consistent pressure on the three slides that do most of the work. A Tuesday is enough.
