Deck hygiene: the five tells.

Five small signals on the first three slides that tell an investor whether the deck was built with care or assembled from a template.

SLATEPRESS · April 23, 2026 · 6 min read

An investor forms a read on a deck in the first thirty seconds. Almost none of that read is about the business. It is about whether the document in front of them looks like the work of a person who cares.

This is the frustrating part of deck work. A founder can have a better business than the one that closed last week and still lose the room because the first three slides look like every deck the investor has seen this year. The signals are small, often unconscious, and sitting in plain view — which means they are also fixable.

Here are the five we check first on any incoming deck. Each is ten minutes of work. None of them changes the business. All of them change the read.

The five tells

1. Two fonts doing the same job

Open the deck. Look at the title slide, then the first body slide. Are the headline and the body using related typefaces — one serif paired with one sans, or one type family with two weights — or are they using fonts that were each chosen on a different Tuesday by a different person? The most common version: a geometric sans like Montserrat on the cover, then a humanist sans like Open Sans on the body slides, with no discernible rationale for the switch.

The fix. Pick one type system and hold it for every slide. A serif display with a neutral sans for body is a reliable choice. A single family in two weights is even simpler. The point is not which fonts you use. The point is that the deck looks like one document written by one person.

2. Logo placement that drifts

Scroll through the first three slides with the thumbnails on. Does the logo sit in the same position at the same size on every slide where it appears, or does it slide around — top-left on the cover, bottom-right on the problem slide, absent on slide three? Drift is the most visible failure of design discipline and the easiest to fix.

The fix. Decide on one position and one size. Either the logo lives in a consistent corner on every slide or it appears only on the cover and the final ask slide. Both are defensible. The drifting placement is not.

3. A title slide that tells you nothing

Read your own title slide out loud. Does it tell an investor what the company does? Most title slides name the company, add a one-word tagline ("Reimagining logistics"), and stop. The investor is then required to keep reading to find out what the company actually is. Most of them won't.

The fix. The title slide should answer, in one sentence, what the business is and who it is for. "Payroll infrastructure for companies hiring their first engineer outside the US." "The operating system for home-health agencies." The company name can be the smaller word on the slide; the sentence is what the investor needs.

4. A traction slide that opens with the graph

Traction slides default to a growth curve in the upper half of the slide with the numbers below it. Investors read the graph first and feel whatever shape it has before they read the supporting numbers. When the graph is flattering, this works. When it is early, inconsistent, or showing a metric the investor hasn't agreed is the right one, it does damage.

The fix. Lead with the sentence, not the shape. "Revenue grew 4x in the last two quarters, from $40K MRR to $160K, with gross margin holding at 78%." The number and the claim land first. The graph supports. The order matters more than the design.

5. A competitor grid that flatters the defaults

The 2x2 where your logo sits alone in the top-right quadrant is a cliche for a reason: it is easy to draw and hard to believe. An investor who has seen forty decks this month has seen thirty-eight of these grids, all with the same geometry. The credibility cost is real even when the underlying claim is true.

The fix. If you actually own a corner of the market, say it in a sentence instead. "We are the only tool in the category that does X, and two of the three largest customers in the category have switched to us in the last year." If the claim needs a grid to be legible, the grid is not wrong — but the grid on its own, with no surrounding argument, is a tell that the founder couldn't articulate the position in prose.

Sharp edge

None of these fixes improve the business. They improve the read of the business. An investor who passes on a great company because the fonts fought each other is making a mistake, but so is a founder who insists the deck shouldn't matter. The deck is part of the pitch. Treating it like a formality is a self-inflicted wound.

How to audit your own deck in ten minutes

Open the deck at 50% zoom so you can see the first three slides as thumbnails on one screen. Squint. What do you see before you read anything — clean rhythm, or visual noise? That squint test is the first filter. Then walk through the five tells above in order. Each one is a binary check. If four of the five come back clean, the deck is in reasonable shape. If three or more come back flagged, the hygiene pass is overdue.

This is not the same as redesigning the deck. Redesign is a much larger job involving narrative, pacing, and specific claims. Hygiene is the thirty minutes of cleanup that happens before the redesign question is even worth asking. It is also the thirty minutes that separates decks that look cared-for from decks that look assembled.
