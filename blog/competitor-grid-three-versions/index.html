Competitor grid: the three versions that work.

The 2x2, the feature matrix, and the we-win-because callout. Which competitor grid to use, in what round, for what competitive landscape.

SLATEPRESS · April 23, 2026 · 6 min read

Every deck has a competitor slide. Almost every competitor slide is one of three shapes, and founders pick the wrong shape roughly three times out of four. The choice is not cosmetic. It decides whether the slide makes the investor confident or nervous, and the default pick, the 2x2 quadrant, is the one that most often makes them nervous.

The competitor slide is doing specific work. It is not there to list everyone in the category. It is there to resolve one question the investor is silently asking on slide seven: "who else is doing this, and why is this team the right bet?" They already know the category has competitors. They are checking whether the founder has a clear-eyed read on the landscape. The slide is a judgment test before it is an information test.

Three formats pass that test. Each fits a different stage and a different shape of market. Pick the wrong one and the slide backfires.

Shape 1 — the 2x2 quadrant

The most-used and most-abused version. Two axes, four quadrants, competitors plotted as dots, your company in the top-right corner. Every founder has seen this slide a thousand times, which is part of the problem.

The 2x2 works when two conditions hold. First, the axes you pick have to be the ones the customer actually evaluates on, not the two that happen to make you look good. "Price" on x and "quality" on y is the telltale sign of a slide built backward from the conclusion. An investor can tell in three seconds whether the axes are real or reverse-engineered.

Second, the category has to be mature enough that a 2x2 actually captures the meaningful variance. In a two-year-old category with eight companies and no settled taxonomy, any 2x2 is a projection. In a mature category like enterprise CRM, observability, or payroll, the 2x2 can carry real information because the axes have been argued over for a decade.

The 2x2 fails when founders use it as a stand-in for thinking. If the axes are vague ("modern vs legacy," "simple vs complex"), the slide is decoration. If you are in the top-right corner and two of your competitors are a single pixel away, the slide is suggesting a distinction that does not hold up. An investor reading this will either ask the question or, worse, decide not to.

Use the 2x2 when the category is mature, the axes are the ones customers actually care about, and you can articulate in one sentence why each competitor sits where they sit. Otherwise, do not use it.

Shape 2 — the feature matrix

Rows for competitors, columns for capabilities, check marks or filled dots in the cells. The version most B2B SaaS founders reach for after the 2x2.

The feature matrix works for one job: resolving a specific "but what about X?" objection the investor has already formed. If you are building in a crowded vertical where three incumbents are known and customers evaluate on a handful of features, the matrix can be the clearest slide in the deck. It puts eight comparisons on one page and lets the investor scan them at glance-speed.

It fails when it becomes exhaustive. Ten rows, fifteen columns, checkmarks everywhere, and the investor cannot read it. They have to trust that your row has the most check marks. That is not a comparison; that is a trust test, and the investor defaults to skeptical. The other failure mode is the feature that is not really a feature ("modern UX," "AI-powered," "customer-first") sitting in the column header as a checkbox your competitors conveniently do not have.

The honest version of this slide is narrow. Three or four competitors, five or six features, and one or two columns where your row is the only one with a check. Those columns must be specific, technical, and verifiable. "Multi-region latency under 40ms," not "fast." "Ships with SOC 2 Type II on day one," not "enterprise-ready."

Use the feature matrix when the buyer is technical, the category has settled on a small set of evaluation criteria, and you have two genuine capability advantages you can name. Otherwise, the slide will be read as a marketing asset, not a product claim.

Shape 3 — the we-win-because callout

No grid. No matrix. One slide, a list of three or four named competitors, and one sentence each about why your company wins against that specific competitor. This is the shape most founders do not try, and it is the one that works best in categories that are either very early or very noisy.

"Acme: we win because their product is built for a different buyer." "Bravo: we win because their unit economics require a far larger sales org to maintain the same revenue." "Circus: we win because they are three years into a platform rewrite that will take two more." Named competitors, named reasons, one line each. The slide reads in fifteen seconds and lands as a founder who knows their landscape better than the investor does.

The shape works because it is honest about what a competitor slide is actually for. The investor is looking for proof that the founder has thought about the landscape. A 2x2 forces that thinking into a shape; the we-win-because slide just shows the thinking. In an early category where the taxonomy is still moving, this is the only format that does not lock you into a claim that will be stale in six months.

It fails when the named reasons are generic. "We win because we are better at UX" is not a reason; it is a wish. Each line must be specific enough that a competitor's founder, reading the slide, would recognize it as their actual constraint.

Use the we-win-because slide when the category is too young for a stable 2x2, the feature set is not what customers buy on, or your edge is structural rather than featural. It is also the safest default at pre-seed, where categories are usually still forming.

Sharp edge

The competitor slide is the place investors reverse-engineer the founder's judgment. A clean 2x2 in a category that does not have clean axes tells the investor the founder is cargo-culting. A sparse we-win-because slide with three sharp reasons tells the investor the founder has the landscape mapped in their head. The format choice is read as a proxy for how the founder actually thinks about competition, and the default choice is usually the worst one.

How to pick

A rough decision rule the studio uses on incoming decks. If the category is more than five years old and has a settled customer taxonomy, a real 2x2 is defensible. Spend an hour interrogating the axes. If you cannot name the top-right company other than yours without hedging, the axes are wrong.

If your buyer is technical and the category has three to five obvious incumbents, a narrow feature matrix with three real differentiators will beat the 2x2 every time. Keep it to six columns. Cut anything that is not verifiable.

If the category is less than three years old, if the taxonomy is still moving, or if your edge is structural (different buyer, different unit economics, different distribution), use the we-win-because slide. This is the default at pre-seed.

The worst version of the competitor slide is not a bad 2x2. It is a 2x2 that should have been a we-win-because slide, built by a founder who reached for the familiar shape without asking whether it fit.
